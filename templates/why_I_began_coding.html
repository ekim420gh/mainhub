Why I began coding...

well, I had no idea what I was getting into. I wanted to try something new.

The story I tell is: I read a Steve Job's biography and Apple's story inspired me to try writing code. I thought I was bright enough to try ... but it didn't take long until I realized how difficult it was to follow and understand instructions. I remember buying access to a tutorial to build a game, but it was too hard. I think it was Andrew Huberman who said that making errors 15% of the time is optimal for learning. Well, at that time I was failing 98% of the time. I wasn't learning much on my own.

Due to a mix of factors I decided to enroll back at Rutgers after a 3-4 year break. I took an intro class about computers and remember learning about bits, pixels, packets, and components of programming using Scratch. For the next few years, I was challenged by math, data structures, algorithms, and just how to assemble things together. In a way, I was forced to read code and make concrete sense out of the abstract. Studying CS, I always felt like the dumbest person in every lecture hall. But, I persisted. Eventually I started building websites and web crawlers.

Fast foward to today, I'm humbled by how far I've come as a programmer. In the beginning I couldn't comfortably print out "Hello World" by myself. Now I'm committing on the daily on GitHub and writing source code for Bloomberg. For the next 3 months I'll be attending lectures and working on assigned projects to increase my knowledge and skills. I'm excited for what is ahead.

I'm proud of the progress, which is slow but steady. Coding is creating. And creating feels good because we're putting life into something.

The more I learn, the more high I feel standing on top of shoulders of giants.

I started coding because I was curious and my curiosity doesn't seem to have an end. Maybe curiosity breeds more curiosity.

I also coded for the money but.. wow, money doesn't fix your problems. That's a level of wealth to realize that, no? I have enough even though I want to accumulate much, much more.

Programming became my time of solitude. It was just me clacking on a keyboard searching for answers on stack overflow. Thinking. Applying. Debugging. Eventually this became fun and as cliche as it may sound, learning to program paved another avenue for self-expression. I want to become more involved with communities though, I can be quite anti-social at times.

Also it feels like I'm building a relationship with computers. That might sound strange, but I feel like I appreciate and admire computing just as a car mechanic might appreciate and admire a car.

Lastly, there is utility in building applications, because innovation affects how we live and interact in our world. Eventually I would love to build useful/fun stuff and teach others who want to learn how to do similar things. That said, I'm still a novice and will be for quite some time. But that's okay. We'll be solving problems for the rest of our lives.

Anyway, why ekim420?
